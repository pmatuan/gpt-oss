5. Circular reasoning: This fallacy occurs when someone uses the conclusion of an argument as a premise. For example, someone might say "The purpose of life is to find meaning, and we find meaning by finding purpose," when in reality the argument is circular and doesn't provide any new information.

By being aware of these fallacies, we can avoid making faulty arguments and instead engage in more productive and meaningful discussions about life purpose and meaning.

**Goal**: Estimate the impact of a personal project or career choice on your sense of purpose and overall life satisfaction.

**Assumptions**:
1. **Personal Fulfillment Score (PFS)**: A subjective measure of how fulfilling you find a particular activity or career. Scale from 0 to 10.
2. **Impact Score (IS)**: A measure of how much you believe your activity or career can positively affect others or the world. Scale from 0 to 10.
3. **Time Commitment (TC)**: The amount of time you plan to dedicate to this activity or career per week. Measured in hours.

**Calculation**:
- **Total Fulfillment (TF)** = PFS  TC
- **Total Impact (TI)** = IS  TC

**Example**:
- Suppose you are considering a career in education.
- **PFS**: 8 (you find teaching highly fulfilling)
- **IS**: 7 (you believe teaching can significantly impact society)
- **TC**: 40 hours per week

**Results**:
- **TF** = 8  40 = 320
- **TI** = 7  40 = 280

**Interpretation**:
- A higher TF suggests that the career will likely bring you personal satisfaction.
- A higher TI indicates a strong potential for societal impact.

**Decision**:
- If both scores are high, this career might align well with your life purpose and meaning. If one score is significantly lower, you might consider adjusting your approach or exploring other options.
